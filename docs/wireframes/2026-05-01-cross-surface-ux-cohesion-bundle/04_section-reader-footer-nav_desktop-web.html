4. Strategy Is Choice

Practitioner bullets · ~2,560 chars · Updated Apr 28, 2026

Strategy is, at its core, an integrated set of choices that uniquely positions a firm in its industry to create sustainable advantage and superior value relative to the competition. Anything less is just planning.

The temptation to substitute completeness for clarity is everywhere. Long lists of "strategic priorities" feel safe — they let everyone see their pet project on the page — but they leave the hard trade-offs unmade and quietly push them down to the front line, where the resources to make them wisely don't exist.

The five-question cascade — winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities required, and management systems — is not a template to fill in. It is a forcing function that makes a leadership team confront the choices it would rather defer.

  • Strategy is a choice, not a wish list — saying yes to one position requires saying no to others.
  • "Where to play" beats "do everything well" — a defined arena makes capabilities and systems coherent.
  • Capabilities and management systems are not afterthoughts; they are how the choice gets executed.
  • Avoiding strategy (operational excellence as a substitute, vision as a substitute, best-practice as a substitute) is the most common failure mode.

4. Strategy Is Choice

Original content · 12,840 chars

To choose is to leave behind. Every strategist who ever made a real difference understood this in their bones, and yet most management teams arrive at the strategy offsite carrying a quiet hope that this year, somehow, they will not have to choose at all.

The discipline of strategy is the discipline of saying no to attractive options so that yes can mean something. A "where to play" that includes every adjacent market is not a where-to-play; it is an abdication dressed up as ambition.

What we have learned, watching dozens of teams attempt this work, is that the cascade only succeeds when leaders treat its five questions as one coherent decision, not five sequential exercises. Capabilities that don't reinforce the where-to-play are theatre; management systems that don't reinforce capabilities are noise.

1. Introduction

Practitioner bullets · ~860 chars · Updated Apr 28, 2026

This book exists because too many leaders mistake activity for strategy. The authors — drawing on a decade reshaping Procter & Gamble — argue that strategy is the answer to five interlocking questions, and that ducking any one of them dooms the rest.

The introduction sets the stakes: in a world that rewards the appearance of progress, the discipline of choice is rare. It is also the only thing that compounds.

  • Strategy = a coordinated set of choices, not a long list of priorities.
  • The five-question cascade will be the spine of the book.
  • Procter & Gamble's turnaround is the running case study.

21. About the Authors

Practitioner bullets · ~640 chars · Updated Apr 28, 2026

A. G. Lafley served two terms as CEO of Procter & Gamble, leading the company through a period of substantial growth and the acquisition of Gillette. He is widely credited with reorienting P&G around consumer-led innovation.

Roger L. Martin is a Professor of Strategic Management at the Rotman School of Management, University of Toronto, where he served as Dean for fourteen years. His work focuses on integrative thinking, design, and corporate strategy.

Together they have spent more than a decade refining the cascade described in this book — first in P&G's boardroom, then in classrooms, and finally in workshops with leadership teams across industries.
