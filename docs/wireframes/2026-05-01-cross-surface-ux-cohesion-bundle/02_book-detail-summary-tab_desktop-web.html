Core thesis

Strategy is not a vision statement, a plan, or operational excellence — it is an integrated set of choices that uniquely positions a firm to create sustainable advantage and superior value. Lafley and Martin argue that most organizations avoid strategy because making real choices is hard; the alternative — doing a bit of everything — guarantees mediocrity. Winning requires clarity about where to play and how to win, reinforced by the right capabilities and management systems.

Five strategic choices

  • Winning aspiration — define what winning looks like in concrete, customer-facing terms, not internal milestones.
  • Where to play — choose the geographies, segments, channels, and product categories you will and will not compete in.
  • How to win — pick a value proposition (cost leadership or differentiation) that wins in those chosen arenas.
  • Capabilities — identify the small set of reinforcing activities the organization must do uniquely well.
  • Management systems — install the structures, measures, and norms that sustain the choices day-to-day.

Practitioner takeaways

  • Treat strategy as a cascade — each choice constrains and informs the next; inconsistency breaks the system.
  • Test choices against the "logic flow" framework before committing: what would have to be true for this to win?
  • Beware the comfort traps: doing nothing, doing everything, playing only to play, or chasing best practice.
