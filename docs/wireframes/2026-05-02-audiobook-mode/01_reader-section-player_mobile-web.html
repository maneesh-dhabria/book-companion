Summary

Kahneman opens by inviting the reader into a deliberate metaphor: the mind operates as if two distinct agents were sharing the steering wheel. System 1 is fast, automatic, and effortless — it recognizes faces, completes the phrase "bread and…", and senses hostility in a voice without any feeling of choice.

System 2 is slow, deliberate, and effortful — it parks in a tight space, fills out a tax form, and compares two washing machines on price-per-load.

The chapter's pivotal demonstration is the Müller-Lyer illusion. Even after you measure the lines and accept that they are equal, your eyes continue to insist one is longer.

Kahneman emphasizes that the two systems are not literal brain regions. They are useful fictions, characters in a story about cognition.

The division of labor is mostly efficient. System 1 handles the routine; trouble arises only in the narrow band where it makes systematic errors and System 2 fails to catch them.
